<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">Tesla Q2 2026 Deliveries: 480,126 — 18% Beat vs ~406K Consensus, +25% Y/Y</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">Tesla Q2 delivers 480,126 vs ~406K consensus — 18% beat, ~25% Y/Y, energy storage 13.5 GWh, full financials July 22.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Tesla's Q2 2026 production, deliveries and deployments 8-K disclosed 451,758 vehicles produced and 480,126 delivered — ~74K above the Tesla-published analyst consensus of 406,024 (~+18% beat) and ~+25% versus Q2 2025's 384,122.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Model 3/Y accounted for 442,936 of production and 467,762 of deliveries; "other models" produced 8,822 and delivered 12,364. Goldman's Mark Delaney and Barclays had both been signaling a beat citing European registrations running +85–90% Y/Y through May and China's refreshed Model Y strength.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
First back-to-back Y/Y growth quarter since the demand slump began — Europe carried the print, offsetting a mid-teens Y/Y decline in US demand after the Q3 2025 tax-credit expiry. The 13.5 GWh energy print keeps the Megapack ramp on track for the July 22 automotive gross margin conversation.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Auto analysts, EV supply-chain desks, and TSLA-holders watching the July 22 full financial print for gross margin confirmation.

<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">U.S. June Jobs Report: NFP +57K Misses +115K Est, Prior Two Months Revised Down 74K</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">BLS June NFP prints +57K vs +115K est — but unemployment ticks down to 4.2% as participation drops and prior months lose 74K in revisions.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
The BLS June 2026 Employment Situation showed nonfarm payrolls grew 57,000 versus the Reuters +110–115K consensus. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2% from 4.3%, but for the "wrong" reason: labor force participation dropped 0.3pp to 61.5%.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Leisure &amp; hospitality shed 61K on weaker seasonal hiring; professional &amp; business services +36K, social assistance +25K, health care +22K. April was revised down 31K to +148K and May down 43K to +129K — a combined 74K haircut. Average hourly earnings rose +0.3% M/M to $37.64, +3.5% Y/Y.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
This is the first labor-side data to cross Chair Warsh's July FOMC. Warsh's June SEP had 9 of 18 dots flipping to at least one hike; a cool NFP + downward revisions cut against that hawkish pivot. The July 29 FOMC hold is now more likely; BofA's "three-hike" call looks harder to defend.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Macro traders, front-end rates desks, and Fed-watchers ahead of July CPI (July 15) and the July 29 rate decision.
</p>

<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">USTR Greer: U.S. Did Not Renew USMCA at July 1 Joint Review — Deal Stays in Force, Annual Reviews Begin</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">USTR Greer says the U.S. did not agree to renew USMCA at its required first joint review — agreement stays in force but on a shrinking runway.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Ambassador Greer's July 1 statement confirmed that at the required first joint review, "The United States did not agree to renew the USMCA in its current form. As a result, the USMCA is not renewed." All three parties met virtually; the agreement remains in force pending resolution or termination.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
USMCA's Article 34.7 mandated a joint review on July 1, 2026 — all three parties either renew for 16 years or trigger annual reviews. The U.S. cited "shortcomings and trade deficits" (~$197B goods deficit with Mexico, ~$46B with Canada). A third bilateral round with Mexico is set for the week of July 20; USMCA sunsets in 2036 if never renegotiated.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Removes the multi-year stability floor for cross-border auto, ag, and energy supply chains. Automakers (GM, F, STLA, TM, Honda) build to USMCA rules-of-origin thresholds; annual-review path introduces revision risk each summer. Tail risk: Section 232 tariff authority layered on top as bilateral leverage.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Trade-policy desks, auto/ag equity teams, and EM-FX macro traders watching peso and CAD volatility around future review windows.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">The 2026 AI-memory darlings take their sharpest hit of the year as a California DRAM lawsuit and Q3 rebalancing bite the winners.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Memory-chip stocks led a broad semiconductor pullback on July 1 — the first trading day of Q3 2026. Micron (MU), SanDisk (SNDK), and Western Digital (WDC) — three of the year's biggest AI-driven winners — dropped 6–11% in a single session as institutional money took profits and a fresh price-fixing complaint hit the tape.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
MU closed $1,032.28 (-10.57%), SNDK $2,032.22 (-10.62%), WDC $598.37 (-6.32%). The overhang: a California class action filed the prior week names Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron as defendants, alleging they "illegally coordinated to restrict DRAM supply and inflate prices" — DRAM contract prices are up ~700% over four years.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Memory has been the hottest AI-adjacent trade of 2026: MU +305%, SNDK +858%, WDC +271% YTD before Wednesday. An 8–11% one-day air pocket on the first day of Q3 is what profit-taking looks like when the tape is that stretched. HBM and enterprise-NAND supply remains sold out through 2026 per Micron's June guide, so the structural bull case is intact — but positioning had to reset.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
