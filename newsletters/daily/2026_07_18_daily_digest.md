<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">Iran struck a Kuwait power and desalination plant in retaliation for US strikes on Iranian bridges; oil closed at one-month highs.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>

</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
The strikes came hours after US forces hit Iranian bridges, an airport, and a railway station. Brent crude rose 4.6% to close at $88.10 and WTI added 4.5% to settle at $82.49 — both at one-month highs, capping a >10% weekly gain.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Attacks on desalination and civilian power open a new escalation lane markets hadn't priced. With crude up >10% for the week alongside the chip-sector rout, the oil–inflation feedback loop is back on the table just as the July 28–29 FOMC approaches.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Energy desks, macro traders, and oil-sensitive credit portfolios watching the FOMC response to renewed inflation pressure.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">

<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">US June 2026 housing starts +19.0% M/M to 1.427M SAAR — biggest monthly jump in years, ~8% above 1.320M consensus; building permits -0.9% to 1.410M</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">US housing starts surged +19.0% M/M in June to a 1.427M annualized rate — the sharpest monthly jump in years — but building permits slipped -0.9% as multi-family softness lingers.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
The Census Bureau and HUD released June 2026 New Residential Construction data at 8:30am ET on Friday July 17. Housing starts hit 1.427M SAAR, up +19.0% from May's revised 1.199M and about 8% above the 1.320M consensus. Building permits printed 1.410M, down 0.9% from May.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
The mix is a two-track story: single-family permits rose +1.2% to 892K annualized while multi-family permits for 5+ unit buildings fell -4.7% to 468K. Midwest permits collapsed -18.9%; the Northeast, South, and West all gained. Favorable weather and pull-forward from a slow earlier start drove the starts beat.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
The +19% starts jump is bullish for homebuilder revenue (LEN, DHI, PHM, TOL, MTH) and is inflationary at the margin via shelter-CPI upstream, nudging rates higher and keeping the July 28–29 FOMC lean-hawkish narrative alive alongside the oil spike.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Macro and rates traders, homebuilder analysts watching DHI and PHM prints on July 22, and FOMC-watchers tracking shelter-CPI inputs.
</p>
