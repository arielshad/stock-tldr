<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">Kevin Warsh sworn in as Fed Chair at the White House — first since Reagan/Greenspan in 1987</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">Warsh takes the Fed gavel at a White House ceremony — symbolism that has Powell-era veterans watching independence cues.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Kevin Warsh was sworn in as the 11th modern-era Federal Reserve Chair in the White House East Room on May 22, with Justice Clarence Thomas administering the oath. It's the first time a sitting Fed Chair has been sworn in at the White House since Reagan's ceremony for Alan Greenspan in 1987.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Warsh was confirmed 54–45 — the narrowest Fed Chair margin on record. Trump said "I want Kevin to be totally independent" at the ceremony, then hours later called for rates to come down "very quickly" at a rally. Jerome Powell stays on as a governor through January 2028.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Warsh inherits a 30-year Treasury yield at 19-year highs, headline CPI at +3.8% Y/Y, and an 8-4 split FOMC (most dissents since October 1992). His first press conference at the June 16-17 FOMC meeting is the first real signal on the rate path.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Macro traders, rates desks, Fed-independence watchers.
</p>

<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">SEC pauses Innovation Exemption for tokenized stocks — Nasdaq, Cboe and CME flag investor-protection gaps; BTC -3.6%, COIN -4%</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">SEC blinks on tokenized stocks — exchanges flag shareholder-rights gaps and the agency holds the Innovation Exemption past its planned May release.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Bloomberg reported that the SEC paused its Innovation Exemption framework for tokenized stocks after meetings with stock-exchange officials raised concerns. The framework had been on track for release "as soon as this week." Bitcoin dropped ~3.6% intra-day below $76K and COIN traded ~4% lower.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
The framework would have created a regulatory sandbox for blockchain-based tokenized equities. The sticking point: a provision allowing third-party tokens — tokenized shares issued without the underlying company's involvement — that Nasdaq, Cboe and CME said creates unresolved dividend, voting, and shareholder rights issues.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Tokenized stocks were the centerpiece of the Atkins-era SEC's "crypto-onto-Wall-Street" agenda. A delay clouds Wall Street partnerships and pushes out the timeline for on-chain RWA infrastructure — COIN, Robinhood, and BlackRock BUIDL-style products all had this as a near-term catalyst.
