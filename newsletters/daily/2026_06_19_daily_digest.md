<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">FOMC June 2026 — Warsh's first meeting holds rates at 3.50–3.75% (12-0) but dot plot pivots from cuts to hikes; 2026 PCE raised to 3.6%</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">Warsh's debut FOMC keeps rates on hold but the dot plot pivots from cuts to hikes as Iran-war energy inflation pushes 2026 PCE to 3.6%.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
The FOMC concluded its June 16–17 meeting — Kevin Warsh's first as Fed Chair — leaving the federal funds target range unchanged at 3.50–3.75% on a unanimous 12-0 vote. The SEP delivered the hawkish surprise: the median 2026 year-end fed funds rate moved to 3.8% from 3.4% in March, implying a 25bp hike rather than the cut March participants had penciled in.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Nine of 18 participants now project at least one rate hike in 2026, with six expecting two 25bp hikes. The statement dropped its cutting bias, added the line "The Committee will deliver price stability," and raised the 2026 PCE projection to 3.6% on Iran-war energy shock and May CPI of 4.2%. Warsh did not submit his own dot, consistent with his stance against Fed forward guidance.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
The 10Y yield jumped ~6bp to ~4.49% and equities sold off (S&P -1.21%, Nasdaq -1.34%). CME FedWatch now prices ~60% odds of at least one hike by year-end — a full ~50bp swing in the median's policy stance from March — handing rate-sensitive sectors a new headwind into the July 28–29 FOMC.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Rates, macro, and equity traders tracking the Warsh-era policy inflection and its effect on long-duration growth names.
</p>
