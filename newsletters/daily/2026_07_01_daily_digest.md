<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">SCOTUS splits the agencies — Fed protected 5-4, Humphrey's Executor overturned 6-3</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">SCOTUS protects the Fed (5-4) while gutting the rest of the independent-agency firewall (6-3) — Humphrey's Executor is dead, but Powell's board is not.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
On Monday June 29 the Supreme Court denied 5-4 the Trump administration's stay in Trump v. Cook, keeping Fed Governor Lisa Cook in her seat, while simultaneously ruling 6-3 in Trump v. Slaughter that the FTC's for-cause removal provision is unconstitutional — overturning the 1935 precedent Humphrey's Executor v. United States.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Chief Justice Roberts explicitly carved the Fed out in Cook, writing that at-will Fed employment would be "out of step with our Nation's tradition of central banking." In Slaughter he wrote the opposite: "The President may remove his subordinates at will" — making the FTC, NLRB, CFTC, and every other independent regulator except the Fed subject to presidential firing.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Markets read the split as preserving monetary-policy independence while expanding executive reach over all other regulators. The S&amp;P 500 closed +0.71% at 7,406.38, Nasdaq +1.29% at 25,623.67, and bond markets rallied on the Fed carve-out.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Macro traders, rates desks, regulatory-policy PMs, anyone trading agency-rule-sensitive sectors (FTC/SEC/NLRB/CFTC targets).
</p>
