<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">China April exports jump 14.1% Y/Y in USD, blowing past 7.9% consensus as factories race to fulfill orders ahead of Trump-Xi summit.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>

</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Trade surplus widened to $84.8B from $51.13B in March. Exports to the U.S. swung from -26.5% in March to +11.3% in April — suggesting front-loading by importers ahead of any Trump-Xi summit outcome.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
The data lands days before the May 14–15 Trump-Xi summit in Beijing, where tariffs and rare earths are on the agenda. Outsized imports growth is read as a sign of stronger China domestic demand.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Macro traders, China-exposed multinationals, commodity desks.

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">Trump gives the EU 57 days to ratify the Turnberry trade deal or watch tariffs spike — autos in the crosshairs at 25%.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>

</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Under the Turnberry deal, the EU agreed to eliminate tariffs on U.S. goods while the U.S. applied a flat 15% on most EU products. Wednesday's talks failed; the next round resumes May 19 with the main sticking point being safeguards against a unilateral U.S. breach.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Germany, Italy and Sweden combined ship roughly $50B+ of vehicles to the U.S. each year. A jump to 25% reintroduces tariff-headline risk for the euro and Stoxx 600 industrials — categories that had largely faded since the Turnberry framework was announced.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Macro traders, EU equity desks, auto analysts.

<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">U.S. consumer sentiment hits record low — U-Michigan May prelim 48.2 (lowest since 1952); gas prices and tariffs cited by ~63% of respondents</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">U-Michigan's May prelim consumer sentiment falls to 48.2 — the lowest reading in the survey's 74-year history.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
The University of Michigan's Surveys of Consumers posted a preliminary May ICS of 48.2, down 3.2% from April's final 49.8 and below the 49.5 consensus. The Current Economic Conditions sub-index plunged 9% month-over-month to 47.8.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
The 48.2 print is the lowest in the data series going back to November 1952 — below readings from the 2008 financial crisis and the 1980 stagflation cycle. About one-third of respondents mentioned gasoline prices and ~30% cited tariffs; one-year inflation expectations eased to 4.5% from 4.7%.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Sub-50 readings have historically aligned with recession periods. Equities shrugged it off Friday on strong April payrolls (+115K), but follow-through into hard data (retail sales, PCE) would pressure consumer-discretionary names and factor into the Fed's dual-mandate calculus.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Macro traders, consumer/retail analysts.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">Upwork prints in line on Q1 but cuts ~145 jobs as AI displaces lower-value gig demand; UPWK -19%.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Upwork reported Q1 2026 revenue of $195.5M (+1.4% Y/Y) and adj EPS $0.35, beating bottom-line consensus. Alongside results, the company laid out a restructuring plan cutting ~24% of its workforce — roughly 145 of ~600 employees.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
AI-related GSV grew >40% Y/Y to over $300M while total GSV came in at $987.1M (~flat Y/Y). Upwork targets ~$70M of annualized savings from $16–$23M in restructuring charges; FY26 adj EBITDA guide was raised to $250–$260M.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
CEO Hayden Brown explicitly attributed the cuts to AI: lower-value freelance work is being absorbed by AI tools. UPWK -19.3% on the day as the market read the Q2 deceleration rather than the EBITDA raise — crystallizing the AI-displacement debate at the demand-side end of the labor market.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Labor-economy and SaaS traders.
