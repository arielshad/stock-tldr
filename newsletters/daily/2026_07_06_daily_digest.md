<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">June NFP +57K misses +115K est — prior two months revised down -74K, unemployment 4.2% as participation drops</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">BLS June NFP prints +57K vs +115K est — but unemployment ticks down to 4.2% as participation drops and prior months lose 74K in revisions.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
The BLS June 2026 Employment Situation showed nonfarm payrolls grew just 57,000 — less than half the Reuters consensus of +115K — while April and May were revised down a combined 74K, bringing the prior 12-month monthly average to +36K.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Leisure &amp; hospitality shed 61K on weak seasonal hiring; professional services (+36K), social assistance (+25K), and health care (+22K) provided modest offsets. Unemployment dipped to 4.2% only because labor-force participation fell 0.3pp to 61.5% — the "wrong reason" decline.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
The miss is the first labor-side data to cross Fed Chair Warsh's desk ahead of the July 29 FOMC, making a hold more comfortable and undermining BofA's "three-hike" call. Front-end rates and rate-sensitive sectors (regionals, homebuilders) typically catch a bid on this shape of print.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Macro traders, front-end rates desks, and Fed-watchers tracking the path to Warsh's July 29 rate decision.
</p>

<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">OPEC+ ratifies fifth straight 188K bpd August quota hike — cumulative April–July adds reach ~800K bpd as Hormuz exports recover, Brent ~$72</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">OPEC+ ratifies fifth straight monthly quota hike as Hormuz shipments start to recover and Brent sits ~40% below war-time peaks.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Seven core OPEC+ producers — Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, Kuwait, Algeria, Kazakhstan, and Oman — held a virtual meeting on Sunday July 5 and agreed to lift collective output targets by another 188,000 bpd for August, a fifth consecutive monthly quota increase. Brent traded near $72/bbl heading into the meeting.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
The August increment matches June and July's hike size, bringing cumulative April–July quota adds to ~800K bpd on paper. The group reportedly weighed a larger 411K bpd step before settling on the smaller increment; physical output collapsed from 42.77M bpd in February to 33.13M bpd in May during the Iran-war Hormuz closure.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Measured 188K increments leave the cartel room to slow if Iranian exports stay patchy, but the direction is clear: OPEC+ is committed to reclaiming lost market share as Hormuz tanker traffic normalizes. Paper quotas have run well ahead of actual barrels shipped during the war disruption.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Energy desks and macro traders watching Brent vs. WTI spreads and Hormuz traffic recovery against OPEC+ quota compliance.
